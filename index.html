BRAILLE

Author note: The following webpage visualizes the notational system of Braille. The irony of this is not lost on me as the author and I have tried to include many features that will make this page accessible to people with vision impairment. As someone who has not needed to rely on Braille as an assistive technology, I felt it it would be insensitive to research this topic without including input from those who are regular users of Braille. I relied on the knowledge of a friend of mine, Megan, who has a visual impairment.

To echo the disability rights movement motto:

"Nothing about us, without us."

Braille is a notational system developed for people with blindness, deafblindness, and low vision. Notational systems represent the sounds or words of a language. In the case of Braille, the notational system is interpreted by the user tactilely instead of visually. While, Frenchman Louis Braille is credited with the development in the early 1800s, Braille was not the first touch-based reading system.

In the late 1900s, Frenchman Valentine Hauy embossed the roman letters in common texts. However, the reliance on typographic composition made the system challenging to learn and resulted in very low literacy among people with low vision. The system which led to the development of Braille was developed by artillery captain Charles Barbier de la Serre in 1822. The code that Barbier developed was known as night writing and developed as a military technology to give soliders the ability to be able to communicate silently and without a light source at night. Barbier's system was phonetic, relied on a 12-dot pattern which did not easily fit under a finger tip pad.

Since Louis Braille's redesign, the language not changed drastically. Since the 1950s there have been efforts to standardize the language by UNESCO. In 2005, the released a statement saying the Braille system is a "vital language of communication, as legitimate as all other languages in the world."

Matrix

Each letter of the alphabet is encoded in a single braille cell. In English Braille, this cell is arranged using 6 dots in a 2 by 3 matrix. In the visual representation to the right, dots which are filled in represent raised dots on a Braille cell.

Megan

A Braille cell contains a-z. It is a dot numbering system that you have to program into your brain. It's about learning these dot positions. It is no bigger than the tip of your finger.

Note: In this view, the cell is enlarged to show the cell numbering system.

Grade 1

Each letter is derived from the Latin alphabet, resulting in a dot pattern system that corresponds to alphabetic letting order within the alphabet. Visually, the system is quite simple, however learning Braille can be extremely challenging.

Megan

It's much harder without vision. You can hear the Braille instructor repeat the dot positions. You can feel them with your hand, but there's nothing like physically seeming something. I have to visualize what I think it may look like and then process what my brain says it looks like.

Note: In this view, the cells are sized to the actual size of a Braille cell.

Braille System

The Braille system is based on the first ten letters in the alphabet A-J. The first ten letters of the alphabet, A-J, only utilize the top four dots in each cell. To create the rest of the alphabet dots A-J are repeated with small changes to create a system.

Grade 2 and 3

Grade 2, letters K through T, makes a slight modification to the system. Each letter uses the same system as the dot above it, but dot five is raised. While the system is visually simple, a Braille user must memorize all 26 lettering positions or remember how one letter is indexically related to another letter. For example, K is related to A..

Assistive technologies are associated with accessibility. However, while Braille does assist many in the visually impaired community, like any technology it isn't accessible to the entire community.
